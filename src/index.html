From Baltimore to Cheltenham

Between 1870 and 1940, more than 230 Black boys died in state custody at the House of Reformation and Instruction for Colored Children at Cheltenham. In 1938, these boys lived here.

177 boys

From a single Annual Report, we reconstructed the Baltimore addresses of 182 boys. Of those, 177 can be placed on the map today.

By 1940, Black boys in Baltimore were more than five times as likely as their white peers to be committed to a state reformatory.

The five that couldn't be found are likely streets erased by mid-century urban renewal — the addresses exist in the record but not on any modern map.

About this map

Based on the 1938 Cheltenham list, transcribed and researched by Capital News Service and the JOUR389/689 class at the University of Maryland's Philip Merrill College of Journalism, with Prof. Rob Wells.

In April 2026, Maryland lawmakers approved legislation creating a state commission to investigate the deaths at Cheltenham. Gov. Wes Moore is expected to sign the bill into law.

Addresses reflect each boy's last known residence before being committed. Some addresses could not be located or verified. The map excludes boys whose pre-incarceration addresses fall outside Baltimore.

Nine times the deaths

Capital News Service identified at least 243 deaths at the all-Black Cheltenham reformatory between 1860 and 1942 — compared to 26 at the state's all-white House of Refuge for Juvenile Delinquents over the same period. At least 97 of those deaths were tuberculosis, a disease the state knew how to treat. Henryton State Hospital, Maryland's only sanatorium for Black patients, refused to accept Cheltenham inmates. “It would be a grave mistake to accept this type of case,” its superintendent wrote in 1929.

Source: Capital News Service analysis of Maryland State Archives death certificates and reformatory annual reports. The count of 243 is expected to rise as research continues.

How we did it

By Zaka Hossain · Capital News Service

Capital News Service reporters investigated Maryland's segregated juvenile justice system across two semesters, from fall 2025 through spring 2026, examining the history of the House of Reformation and Instruction for Colored Boys at Cheltenham alongside the House of Refuge for Juvenile Delinquents (now the Charles H. Hickey, Jr. School) and the Maryland House of Correction at Jessup.

Archives and libraries

Reporters made three visits to the Maryland State Archives in Annapolis, where they reviewed annual and biennial reports, grand jury working papers and administrative records dating to the late 19th century. Additional research was conducted at the Thurgood Marshall State Law Library, the University of Baltimore Special Collections, the Enoch Pratt Free Library, the University of Maryland's Hornbake Library and the Baltimore City Archives. Reporters also searched the NAACP's online archival records, uncovering correspondence related to Thurgood Marshall's advocacy for Cheltenham boys.

Data and digital tools

Scanned death certificates containing handwritten text were processed using a Python script and Google Gemini to extract structured data, which reporters verified against source records and analyzed in R. Reporters analyzed more than 1,000 articles from The Baltimore Sun and the Afro-American spanning roughly eight decades of the institution's operation. Visualizations were built in Flourish and Datawrapper. Google Pinpoint organized and searched large volumes of scanned historical documents. Claude was used to assist with research synthesis.

Interviews

Reporters interviewed state officials, current and former Maryland lawmakers, juvenile justice researchers, public health experts, historians and community advocates from across the country.

Genealogical research

To reconstruct the lives of boys incarcerated at Cheltenham, reporters used Ancestry.com and FamilySearch to search census records, death certificates, draft records and institutional intake documents. Reporters also contacted and interviewed descendants of former Cheltenham inmates, whose family histories added a personal dimension to the documentary record.

Capital News Service support

Alex Taylor from the CNS Annapolis bureau covered the state legislative developments related to Cheltenham. The project was led by Rob Wells, Ph.D., associate professor, with assistance from faculty and staff: Deborah Berry, Stacey Decker, Sean Mussenden and Josh Land.
