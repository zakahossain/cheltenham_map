From Baltimore to Cheltenham

Between 1870 and 1940, more than 230 Black boys died in state custody at the House of Reformation and Instruction for Colored Children at Cheltenham. In 1938, these boys lived here.

1938

Each puck marks a last known Baltimore address — a home a boy left when the state sent him to Cheltenham.

Three of the boys shown here were dead within a year of this list. One would spend a decade in Army detention. Another arrived at age 12 and was in the Maryland Penitentiary by 19.

177 boys

From a single Annual Report, we reconstructed the Baltimore addresses of 182 boys. Of those, 177 addresses can be placed on the map today.

The five that couldn't be found are likely streets erased by mid-century urban renewal — the addresses exist in the record but not on any modern map.

The path to Cheltenham

The reformatory sat 30 miles south of Washington, D.C., in Prince George's County — just outside the capital's shadow.

Beside the base

The House of Reformation and Instruction for Colored Children stood south of what is now Joint Base Andrews -- the home base of Air Force One.

The wooded cemetery

Some boys never left. Their graves lie in a wooded cemetery adjacent to the Cheltenham Veterans Cemetery.

About this map

Based on the 1938 Cheltenham list, transcribed and researched by Capital News Service and the JOUR389/689 class at the University of Maryland's Philip Merrill College of Journalism, with Prof. Rob Wells.

Addresses reflect each boy's last known residence before being committed. Some addresses could not be located or verified. The map excludes boys whose pre-incarceration addresses fall outside Baltimore city.
